About

My Journey

2018–2019 — Mechanical Engineering (Foundation)
Internship at a Refinery Port introduced me to industrial safety. Researching incidents like Piper Alpha sparked a lasting interest. I completed a Diploma in Industrial Safety alongside my degree.

2020 — NEBOSH & first site role
Mentored by Mr. Anees, I pursued NEBOSH IGC and passed on my first attempt (Feb 2020). I joined a Thermal Power Plant as Safety Officer—gaining practical experience before COVID-19 halted work.

2021–2023 — Shankar Electricals (major growth)
Deployed to a Data Centre construction project in Mumbai. After two months my senior resigned and I took full responsibility, eventually leading safety for ~180+ workers. I halted unsafe lifts (e.g., a 7.4-ton transformer with an uninspected crane; a night tower-crane drum lift with damaged slings) and reset permit discipline. Earned Best Performer and later handled five projects.

2023–May 2025 — Contract & remote roles
On a 3-month contract I built procedures, ran worker assessments, and issued online certifications. Later I supported a remote program validating AI-captured safety observations and performing digital risk assessments.

June 2025–Present — UK transition & CPD
Moved to the UK on 13 June 2025 and aligned with UK standards: COSHH, HSWA 1974, and ISO 45001 (Lead Auditor training). Achieved TechIOSH. 2025 CPDs include: Diploma in Environmental Management, Behaviour-Based Safety, and Environmental Management & Sustainability (UKAF).

Safety Insights

The growth of safety: from industry to modern era

Safety evolved from minimal protections during the Industrial Revolution to today’s integrated systems. Organisations now recognise safety as an investment in people, reliability, and delivery—not a cost.

Hazards then vs now

  • Then: poorly guarded machinery, explosions, poor ventilation, child labour, no PPE.
  • Now: chemical exposure & COSHH control, electrical & fire, ergonomics, psychosocial stress, digital fatigue, and risks from automation/AI.

Global gap & collective responsibility

In some regions, weak enforcement means safety is still treated as optional—leading to preventable incidents. Sustainable improvement comes when everyone accepts duty of care: workers, supervisors, designers, suppliers, and leadership.

Case Studies

⚠️ Piper Alpha (1988)

The North Sea oil platform disaster highlighted the catastrophic consequences of poor permit-to-work systems and inadequate emergency preparedness. It reshaped offshore safety regulations worldwide. Read more →

☣️ Bhopal Gas Tragedy (1984)

One of the world’s worst industrial disasters, caused by a toxic gas leak in India, revealed how weak maintenance, training, and corporate responsibility can devastate entire communities. Read more →

🏗️ Rana Plaza Collapse (2013)

The garment factory collapse in Bangladesh demonstrated the dangers of ignoring structural safety and the human cost of prioritising deadlines over worker welfare. It accelerated global supply chain audits and ethical sourcing initiatives. Read more →
